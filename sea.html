Jurmala Beach Activities

The Jurmala beach is the longest white-sand stretch on the Latvian coast — 33 kilometres of fine quartz sand, shallow Baltic water and pine dunes. This page is a guide to the Jurmala beach activities visitors actually do, ordered roughly from the calmest to the most active.

Most of the beach activities below cluster between the Lielupe and Jaunkemeri stations of the suburban train line. The busiest section is in front of Jomas iela in Majori, which has lifeguards, beach volleyball nets, public toilets and showers, and a paved promenade. The quieter sections, towards Vaivari and Jaunkemeri, are better for walking, kitesurfing and birdwatching.

Swimming in the Baltic Sea

The water is shallow — you can wade out fifty metres and still be at chest height — which makes the Jurmala beach unusually safe for families with small children. Sea temperatures reach 20 to 23 degrees Celsius in July and August, and lifeguards are on duty along the central beach from mid-June to late August. Blue-flag certification covers the main bathing areas in Majori, Bulduri and Dzintari.

Two practical notes for swimmers. First, the beach has almost no waves on most days; if you are used to Mediterranean or Atlantic surf, the calm here can be surprising. Second, water quality is checked weekly during summer and is published by the Latvian Centre for Disease Prevention and Control — after heavy rain it occasionally drops, so it is worth checking before swimming with children.

Sunbathing on the white sand

The sand is the reason most people come. It is fine, almost-white quartz, soft underfoot, and the dunes behind it are stabilised by pine forest and dune grass — which means there is shade five metres back from the water if you want it. Sunbeds and umbrellas can be rented in the Majori and Dzintari sections; further along the beach, you bring your own towel.

Beach volleyball and other ball games

Permanent volleyball nets are set up in front of Majori and Dzintari throughout summer. They are first-come, first-served and free to use; rackets and balls can be rented from beach kiosks. The hard-packed sand near the waterline is firm enough for football, frisbee and kite-flying, and there is usually a casual game in progress on weekend afternoons.

Kitesurfing and windsurfing

The prevailing summer wind blows along the coast from the west, which is ideal for kitesurfing and windsurfing. The launching point most riders use is in Lielupe at the river mouth, where the Lielupe river meets the Gulf of Riga and the wind is steadiest. There are two schools renting boards and offering lessons in English. Wetsuits are recommended even in July — the water is bracing once you are wet and on the wind.

Walking the shoreline

Walking the full length of the Jurmala beach takes most of a day at a relaxed pace. Many visitors do shorter sections — Majori to Dzintari is about 3 kilometres and takes 45 minutes, with cafés and ice cream stands along the way. Early morning is the most rewarding time: the sand is firm, the light is low, and the only company is people walking dogs and the occasional jogger.

For a longer route, you can pick up the wooden boardwalk that climbs into Dzintaru Mezaparks directly from the Dzintari beach exit. The combination of beach walk and pine forest is one of the most popular routes for visitors with half a day to spend.

Sunsets and photography

Jurmala faces north-west, so the sun sets over the sea from late spring through early autumn. The best photographic spot is in front of Jurmalas Brivibas iela in Majori, where the curve of the coast lets you frame the pier in silhouette. In June and early July, sunsets are unusually long — civil twilight stretches past eleven at night.

Activities for children

The shallow water is the obvious draw for families, but there is more. A small playground sits next to the Majori beach exit, and pedal carts can be rented at the entrance to the beach in Bulduri. For a rainy day, the indoor pools at Livu Akvaparks are a five-minute drive from the central beach.

Beach amenities and practical information

  • Lifeguards: on duty in Majori, Bulduri and Dzintari from mid-June to late August, 11:00 to 19:00.
  • Toilets and showers: public facilities at every train-station beach exit.
  • Food and drink: kiosks along the beach in summer; full restaurants on Jomas iela two blocks back.
  • Parking: in summer, cars need a one-day Jurmala entry pass (€2). Buy online or at the entry barriers.
  • Dogs: permitted on most of the beach but not in the marked central bathing zone between June and August.

When to go

For active beach activities — swimming, volleyball, kitesurfing — July and August are the obvious months. For walking, photography and birdwatching, September is the best of the year: the crowds have left, the water is still warm enough to wade in, and the light turns golden in the afternoons. Winter visits are quiet and atmospheric but most beach amenities are closed.

Combining the beach with other activities

Most visitors pair the beach with one or two other Jurmala activities in the same day. Common combinations:

  • Morning swim, lunch on Jomas iela, afternoon at Dzintaru Mezaparks.
  • Beach in the morning, Jurmala Open-Air Museum in the afternoon — both in walking distance of each other in Lielupe.
  • Beach during the day, evening concert at Dzintari Concert Hall.
